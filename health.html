HEALTH AND SAFETY

Pregnancy Health

Here's a short and concise list of important points
about health during pregnancy:
Regular prenatal check-ups with a healthcare provider
are essential.
Eat a balanced and nutritious diet, rich in fruits,
vegetables,whole grains,lean proteins,and dairy products
Stay hydrated by drinking plenty of water.
Monitor weight gain to ensure it's within a healthy range.
Engage in moderate and safe exercise with approval
from a healthcare provider.
Avoid smoking, alcohol, and illicit drugs entirely.
Take prenatal vitamins as prescribed, including folic acid,
iron, and calcium.
Manage stress and get enough rest.
Get recommended vaccinations, such as flu and Tdap vaccines.
Educate yourself about pregnancy and labor.
Monitor prenatal development through ultrasounds and tests.
Be aware of warning signs and seek medical attention for any
concerning symptoms.

Test and Screening

Ultrasound: Uses sound waves to create images of the
developing fetus
Blood Pressure Monitoring: Regular checks to detect
gestational hypertension or preeclampsia.
lood Tests: Assess blood type, anemia, immunity to diseases,
and screen for gestational diabetes.
Urinalysis: Checks for infections, kidney problems,
or signs of preeclampsia.
Glucose Challenge Test: Screens for gestational diabetes.
Group B Streptococcus (GBS) Test: Identifies GBS bacteria in
the mother, which can be passed to the baby during childbirth.
Non-Invasive Prenatal Testing (NIPT): Screens for genetic
disorders by analyzing fetal DNA in the mother's blood.
Chorionic Villus Sampling (CVS) or Amniocentesis: Diagnostic
tests for chromosomal abnormalities or genetic disorders.
Fetal Non-Stress Test (NST): Monitors the baby's heart rate in
response to movement.
Anatomy Ultrasound: Detailed ultrasound to check the baby's
anatomy and development.

Weight to gain during Pregnancy

The recommended amount of weight gain during pregnancy
can vary depending on a woman's pre-pregnancy body mass
index.

Underweight (BMI less than 18.5): Recommended weight
gain is about 12.5 to 18 kilograms.
Normal weight (BMI between 18.5 and 24.9): Recommended
weight gain is about 11.5 to 16 kilograms.
Overweight (BMI between 25 and 29.9): Recommended weight
gain is about 7 to 11.5 kilograms.
Obese (BMI greater than 30): Recommended weight gain is about
5 to 9 kilograms.

Vaccines you need

The following vaccines are commonly recommended for pregnant women:

Influenza (Flu) Vaccine: The flu vaccine is recommended for pregnant
women, especially during the flu season (typically from October to
March in the Northern Hemisphere). Pregnant women are at an
increased risk of severe complications from the flu, so the vaccine
can help protect both the mother and the baby.

Tdap Vaccine: The Tdap vaccine protects against tetanus, diphtheria,
and pertussis (whooping cough). It is recommended during each pregnancy,
ideally between 27 and 36 weeks of gestation. This vaccine helps
provide the baby with some protection against pertussis before they
are old enough to receive their vaccines.

Medications that are Safe

Prenatal vitamins: These are specially formulated multivitamins
designed for pregnant women and typically contain essential
nutrients like folic acid, iron, calcium, and others that
support the health of the mother and baby.
Acetaminophen (Tylenol): It is generally considered safe for
relieving mild to moderate pain or reducing fever during pregnancy.
Always use it under medical supervision and avoid exceeding the
recommended dosage.
Some antibiotics: Certain antibiotics may be prescribed for treating
bacterial infections if necessary. Your healthcare provider will determine
the most appropriate option based on your condition.
Antacids: These may be used to relieve heartburn and indigestion
in pregnant women under medical guidance.
Topical creams and ointments: Some topical treatments may be considered
safe for use during pregnancy to address skin conditions or mild pain.

It's crucial to avoid certain medications during pregnancy, including
some over-the-counter medications and most prescription medications
that are not specifically deemed safe during pregnancy.
